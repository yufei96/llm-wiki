# SCARS (Simulator Common Architecture and Resource Sharing) Initiative — 2018

## Overview & Purpose

**SCARS** is a U.S. Air Force sustainment initiative to incrementally establish a **common, open-systems architecture** for Air Force simulators. Its goals are to:

- Improve **cyber resilience**
- Increase **responsiveness**
- Minimize **life-cycle costs**

> **Definition:** "SCARS is a sustainment initiative to incrementally establish a common open-systems architecture for Air Force simulators in order to improve cyber resilience, responsiveness, and minimize life cycle costs."

## Current State & Problem (as of 2018)

- **Scale:** Over 2,380 training devices across 50+ platforms supporting 9 MAJCOMs (Active, Guard, Reserve, Air & SOF).
- **Challenges:**
  - Multiple program obsolescence states (hardware, software, cyber).
  - Individual, ad-hoc security baselines.
  - 50+ different configurations for Live-Virtual-Constructive (LVC) training.
  - Labor-intensive Risk Management Framework (RMF) reviews.
  - Outdated threat signatures on some simulators.

## SCARS Value Proposition (Before vs. After)

| **OTI Before SCARS** | **OTI After SCARS** |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Individual ad hoc security baselines | Well-known standardized security baseline |
| 50+ different LVC configurations | Common configurations for easier LVC implementation |
| Outdated threat signatures on some sims | Common mission rehearsal baseline (same threat signatures) |
| Concurrency reliant on MAJCOM investment | Increased MAJCOM leverage of enterprise investments |
| Labor-intensive RMF review/oversight | Focused RMF review on specific risks/unique functions |

## Core Architecture & Approach

- **Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA):** Executes DoD MOSA to move away from vendor lock and increase reuse.
- **Simulator Common Architecture (SCA):** The defined common architecture using both common and open standards.
- **Incremental Implementation:** Annual release of common standards to reduce risk, allow innovation, and adapt to fiscal constraints.
- **Transition Methods:**
  1. Incremental modifications to existing training devices.
  2. Leveraging Training System support contract re-competitions.
  3. Recapitalizing/re-hosting training systems.

## Key Components of the SCARS End State

1. **SCARS Prime:** The central hub providing management services, a Security Operations Center (SOC), a testbed library, and common application servers.
2. **SCARS Affiliates:** Weapon System Training Systems that connect to the SCA.
3. **SCARS Partners:** Network providers (e.g., WAN contractors).
4. **Centralized Functions:** Include a patch repository, application management, and the SOC for remote scanning, updates, and support.

## First Standard & Implementation Roadmap

- **First Standard (SMP17): Remote Scanning.** Uses ACAS scanning software for enterprise-wide RMF control.
- **Roadmap (FY17-FY24):**
  - **Increment 1:** Remote Scanning (SMP17) implementation.
  - **Increment 2:** Additional SMP17 platforms and initial SCARS capability.
  - **Increment 3:** SCARS Prime Contract award and implementation.
  - **Ongoing:** SCARS Prime Contract (5-year base + 5 one-year options), Weapon System TS Contracts (Affiliates), and WAN Contracts (Partners).

## Industry Opportunities

- **Prime Contract:** Estimated value of **$200-300M over 5 years** and **$700-900M over 10 years**. Solicitation available on FedBizOps (search "SCARS").
- **Training System Contracts:** MOSA & SCARS standards will be increasingly important.
- **Other Opportunities:** Development of common tools, databases, and applications.
