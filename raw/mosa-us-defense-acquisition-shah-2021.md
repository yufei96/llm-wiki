# Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA) and Its Impact on US Defense Acquisition

> **Author:** Parth Devang Shah. George Washington University, D.Eng. Praxis, 2021
> **Source:** raw/mosa-us-defense-acquisition-shah-2021.pdf (full thesis PDF)
> **Ingested:** 2026-04-05

## Abstract

Almost 60% of US Major Defense Acquisition Programs (MDAPs) are over budget or delayed. The DoD mandated MOSA via Better Buying Power 3.0 in 2015 to combat technology obsolescence, upgrade cohesive systems, and increase flexibility and innovation while battling cost/schedule challenges. Despite multi-year investment, there is no clear qualitative evidence showing MOSA's success on reducing MDAP overruns. This thesis provides that evidence (largely qualitative) and suggests how MOSA supporters can make its effects more effective.

## Key Findings (Chapter 1)

### Problem Context
- **60% of MDAPs** are over budget or behind schedule
- Pre-2010 MDAPs: **62.4% cost growth** ($542.1B), average **35-month delays**
- Post-2010 MDAPs: **2.1% cost growth** ($5.3B), average **9-month delays** (but many still in early phases)
- GAO 86 major programs total cost: **$1.66 trillion**
- Technology obsolescence costs DoD **$750M annually**

### What MOSA Addresses
- **Vendor lock**: IP ownership prevents switching service providers
- **Platform transformability**: systems operating well beyond planned service life, used outside original scope
- Need for adaptable, open, modular systems that are multidomain

### Core Definitions (from Ch1)
- **MOSA**: Acquisition approach mandating modular design, openly published interfaces, and government data rights
- **Modularity**: Architectural principle dividing systems into discrete, interoperable components with well-defined interfaces
- **Open Systems**: Use of publicly available, consensus-based standards for interoperability across vendors
- **MDAP**: Major Defense Acquisition Program (≥$480M per fiscal year)

### Research Structure
- **H1**: MOSA cost/schedule effects
- **H2**: MOSA as acquisition strategy
- **H3/H4**: US vs non-US government impacts
- **H5/H6**: Commercial sector spillover effects
- Case studies: US Army, US Navy, DoD Avionics, global defense, commercial crossover

### Key Architectural Frameworks (from TOC)
- **FACE™** (Future Airborne Capability Environment) - The Open Group
- **OMS** (Open Mission Systems) - DoD
- **UCS** (Unmanned Systems Control Segment)
- **SOSA**, **CMOSS**, **VICTORY** (referenced in scope)

### Related: Android Analogy (Research Motivation §1.2)
Shah draws parallel to Android's open-source approach - initial skepticism → massive ecosystem of allied developers building applications. Suggests same pattern should apply to defense: shift from hardware-based to software-based systems supporting real-time upgrades.

### Problem Statement (verbatim)
> "Despite a huge, multiyear investment in MOSA, its supporters are unable to demonstrate its value in a convincing way potentially threatening its continued use as a promising design and acquisition strategy."
