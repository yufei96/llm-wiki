# Navy to Take More Control Over Open Architecture Development

## Key Change
The U.S. Navy is shifting its approach to managing open architectures by authoring the initial version of its Open Systems Management Plan (OSMP) internally, rather than relying on contractors to produce the first draft.

## Rationale & Historical Context
- Previous Approach: The OSMP was historically "handed off to contractors as a check-the-box exercise," often becoming "shelfware" (unused documentation).
- New Goal: By writing the first version, the Navy aims to embed its priorities from the outset, establishing a foundational "blueprint" or "DNA" for system structure.

## Core Objectives of the New OSMP
The internally-authored OSMP will emphasize:
- Modularity
- Standards compliance
- Lifecycle affordability
- Reuse

## Key Quotes & Insights from Rich Ernst (Senior Open Architecture SME)
- On the OSMP's new role: It will be treated as a "living" reference to guide programs throughout their lifecycle, not a static document.
- On preserving value: The intent is to capture architectural decisions at inception and prevent later program offices from "trading away value."
- On continuity: The plan ensures continuity of architectural principles as personnel change over decades of platform sustainment.
- On benefits: The approach helps program offices:
  - Identify key interfaces.
  - Align with evolving standards.
  - Leverage R&D to incorporate new technologies.
- On industry impact: Standardizing decomposition and reuse strategies aims to create opportunities for small businesses and nontraditional suppliers to contribute modules across multiple programs and services.

## Strategic Alignment
The effort is designed to build an enduring framework supporting:
- Continuous competition
- Diversified suppliers
- More resilient system designs
- Alignment with Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA) principles
