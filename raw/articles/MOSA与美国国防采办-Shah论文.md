Abstract of Praxis 
 
 
Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA) and its Impact on the US Defense 
Acquisition Methods and Programs 
 
Almost 60% of the US Major Defense Acquisition Programs are either over budget or 
delayed in terms of schedule. To combat and address this, the Department of Defense has 
introduced and mandated the implementation of the Modular Open Systems Approach 
through the Better Buying Power 3.0 directive in 2015. This strategy will hope to combat 
technology obsolescence, upgrade current highly cohesive systems, and increase 
flexibility and innovation all the while trying to battle cost and schedule challenges. 
The US Defense industry is arguably undergoing its greatest transformation ever. 
Under strong pressure to decrease costs, accelerate the speed of product development, 
promote innovation, and enhance quality, the US Defense Department has been searching 
for new methods to transform its defense acquisition process. The US Department of 
Defense is the largest defense organization in the world, responsible for spending about 
$989 billion annually on technology acquisition. In fact, “the United States spends more 
on defense than the next nine countries combined” (Kimberly, 2019).    
Several years in, there is no evidence showcasing that this strategy is successful. This 
research alone gathers this evidence, albeit largely qualitative, and suggests how modular 
and open system supporters and contributors can make its effects more effective so that 
this promising approach can be further sustained. This research will benefit students and 
practitioners of systems engineering, stakeholders across government, industry, and 
academia that are affiliated with the production, quality, and manufacturing fields to 
promote a collaborative vision of better acquisition processes and program success.

Chapter 1 - Introduction 
 
 
1.1 Background 
 
The speed of change coupled with balancing emerging threats, technology 
obsolescence, rapidly evolving technology, and increased security requirements has 
required the U.S. Department of Defense (DoD) to change the way it acquires defense 
systems. The DoD has had to move away from traditional acquisition methods to 
embrace newer modular and open system methods.  Recognizing the need for affordable 
solutions and achieving time and schedule targets, the DoD calls for the application of the 
Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA). MOSA’s purpose of preventing vendor lock, 
supporting affordable capability evolution, and encouraging innovation has made it an 
attractive method to acquire current and future defense systems. Vendor lock refers to 
restrictions that prevent the DoD from switching from one service provider to another. 
The company that built the defense systems holds the intellectual property rights for the 
system, thus can be the only service provider preventing switching to an alternate vendor.   
In the US DoD, defense systems tend to frequently operate well beyond their initial 
planned service life and services use these systems in functions outside of their original 
scope or design. Systems that demonstrate some nonfunctional attributes such as 
performance, maintainability, and usability achieve platform transformability and 
continue to serve the DoD in spite of the changes to their operating environment. 
Nevertheless, the DoD does retire systems that cannot be upgraded due to vendor lock 
design constraints or affordability concerns or are unable to meet new functional 
requirements in new operational environments. Over time, as program and mission 
requirements change, threats increase and expectations grow, defense systems will be 
2 
 
expected to adapt be able to upgrade accordingly or systems that do comply to this will 
eventually be retired out of service. This is where modularity and MOSA play an integral 
part.  
Today, many organizations are facing increased business uncertainty as they 
encounter innovative competitors. “Identifying new opportunities and strategically 
transforming to take advantage of these opportunities is a key to the long-term survival of 
a firm” (Ramesh & Delen, 2019). The United States is the top spender on defense and 
military equipment in the world and used to be known as the forefront in defense 
innovation. The DoD is responsible for spending over $989 billion annually on defense 
technology through the employment of thousands of acquisition projects and people and 
thus not only survival but transformation is vital for it to grow. “The military enterprise 
substantively and directly affects the national economy and thus it is fitting to expect that 
military process improvement can have direct impacts on general civilian enterprises” 
(Badiru & Thomas, 2013). Currently, the DoD manages multiple projects. The largest 
projects managed are called Major Defense Acquisition Programs (MDAP) and projects 
of this category each meet or exceed $480 million of total expenditures within a fiscal 
year. The United States Government Accountability Office (GAO) investigates and 
releases an annual report with respect to the cost and schedule performance of these 
programs and the impacts of cost and schedule overruns on these MDAPs. The GAO 
found an interesting trend when looking at the 86 MDAP programs (Appendix A). From 
these programs, the GAO found that the 61, that were all started before 2010 faced cost 
growth of 62.4 percent or $542.1 Billion and schedule overruns of 35 months on an 
average. The remaining 25 programs that were initiated after 2010 observed a cost 
3 
 
growth of 2.1 percent or $5.3 billion and schedule overruns of nine months as compared 
to the original estimates. Vital to note is that this trend may change over time as many of 
the programs after 2010 are now in the very early production stages or yet to enter the 
production phase. For a matter of perspective, in their 2017 report dated April 25, 2018, 
the GAO listed 86 major programs accounting for a cost a total cost of $1.66 trillion. 
(GAO, 2018).  
The research in the Praxis addresses the question of whether there is a relationship 
between MOSA and program success by critically examining the impact of MOSA on the 
defense acquisition process through various means. The Praxis begins with a focus on a 
deeper understanding of modularity and open systems, and their role as a strategy within 
the DoD. The Praxis will then further highlight case studies from commercial, non-
governmental, and other global governmental department of defense lenses. There will be 
a deeper dive into avionic systems that forms a compelling example within the DoD 
where MOSA is actively being pursued in the advancement and development of modular 
systems and has experienced a high rate of success in integrating modularity.  
Consequently, this Praxis will focus on the standards of implementing modularity that are 
being developed and used in avionic systems. Lastly, this research will address the 
emergence of MOSA worldwide and its success leading to sponsored studies and 
partnerships by the UK Ministry of Defence (MOD), Australian DoD, and the Canadian 
Armed Forces (CAF).  All of these organizations now advocate for the benefits of more 
open technical and business practices.

1.2 Research Motivation 
The DoD needs to move away from the archaic system of buying hardware defense 
systems that do not communicate with one another and are specific to certain mission 
needs. While that strategy worked for decades, the game has changed.  
When the Android platform was introduced, there was a lot of skepticism of its open-
source approach. Fast forward to a few years later, the open system approach led to 
creative alliances of individuals and organizations to build applications that have become 
vital and are found in billions of personal devices. From maps to entertainment tools, 
there is an application for everything. The lessons learned can be applied to defense 
systems too.  
To compete and be a leader today, there is a need for adoption of buying hardware-
based defense systems to software-based systems that support real time upgrades through 
software. DoD’s future success will lie on adaptable, open, and modular defense systems 
that are multidomain and can interact with multiple defense systems to provide an edge in 
decision making.   
The US DoD has adopted the use of open and modular systems under a strategy 
called MOSA. Currently there is no specific study evaluating the cost effectiveness and 
other impacts of modularity or open architecture within the DoD acquisition strategies. 
There is also a lack of material showing strong evidence of modularity being a direct 
causal variable in the success or failure of a defense acquisition program. 
Defense weapon acquisitions usually generate highly complex systems with multiple 
stakeholders. These stakeholders typically span from multiple locations and organizations 
collaborating to manage changes in requirements and scope. These efforts lead to 
5 
 
delivering major defense systems such as weapons, aircraft, submarines, satellites, outer 
space technology, and so on. The GAO is responsible for investigating the performance 
of these programs in terms of schedule and cost and their impact on the US Government. 
It is often observed that as systems get complex, there is a higher susceptibility for these 
programs to exceed their initial cost and schedule targets. “US Government 
Accountability Office (GAO) reports identified a range of possible root causes for the 
overruns from the structure of internal open approach policies for each of the military 
services (Army, Navy, Air Force), to the lack of expertise in facilitating an open systems 
approach” (GAO, 2013). The GAO’s 2017 report further helped identify whether 
adopting MOSA has aided in reducing these overruns or has led to a major impact. 
“Major defense acquisition programs (MDAPs) are notoriously prone to substantial cost 
overruns, and engineering and design issues are often partially to blame.”(Valerdi, 
Dabkowski, & Dixit 2015).  
Obsolescence (of systems) is a huge problem costing the DoD an average of $750 
million annually - an alarming number. (Adetunji, Bischoff, & Willy 2018). Due in part 
to the lack of the knowledge needed to isolate the effects of modularity on programs and 
an inability to acquire detailed performance on MDAPs, there has not been significant 
analysis or studies performed with respect to modularity and its effects within the DoD. 
Studying the defense acquisition programs should allow a better understanding of the 
performance of defense acquisition programs over the years and help form a framework 
to better forecast costs and schedules.

1.6 Scope of Research 
The extent of the research for this effort focuses on modular and open systems 
integration through the MOSA strategy or defense acquisition programs. The research 
focuses on the performance aspect of the defense acquisition programs with respect to 
cost and schedule. Considering there are several variables and contemporary acquisition 
trends such as concurrent engineering, lean and agile methods, smart manufacturing, 
service orientation, Model Based Systems Engineering, and autonomy that are being used 
to support defense acquisition programs today, more insight could be gained if there was 
a way to account for the independent effects of each of these variables and to compare 
each influence against the others. This would require more time (and information) than is 
available to this study. Instead, to fit the research study to its limited time span, the 
research was narrowed to focus only on modular and open system architecture methods.  
1.7 Research Limitations 
Modularity, open architecture, and MOSA are all very broad terms that can lead to 
various interpretations and this can limit the direct comparison of programs with respect 
to cost and schedule performance. The scope of this effort is broad because there is only 
limited information with respect to MOSA. It also turns out that there is only limited 
information collected. Further, there may be programs within the DoD that have already 
applied some level of modularity or are currently applying modular approaches using 
different terminology. The US DoD is the largest defense engineering organization in the 
world. Due to this, there are a lot of great processes, expertise, and high-level system 
engineers that create large scale, interdependent, and highly complex defense systems. 
8 
 
This creates a huge limitation as a lot of information exists between contractors, vendors, 
defense program leads, project managers and others, thus multiplying efforts on getting 
single source information about program performance. MOSA is one activity that exists 
to assist with building successful highly technological defense systems while trying to 
balance schedule and cost goals. Due to the limitations placed on US DoD programmatic 
information available to private citizens, not all the information needed to study MOSA’s 
impact on programs is readily available. There might be classified MOSA program 
related information. Thus, it becomes tougher to regularly compare the most updated 
version of the project performance data for comparison.  
Another limitation of this research is that it is almost impossible to attribute MOSA as 
the direct factor in the project performance of a defense acquisition program. Since the 
nature of defense programs and their acquisition cycles can be multiyear (and usually 
consist of multiple sub-systems), validating and gaging the impact of the MOSA strategy 
becomes even more challenging. Consequently, a goal of this praxis is to also shed light 
on how this comparison can be done better so that the positive influence of MOSA can be 
effectively measured.

2.2 Modularity 
Modularity can be introduced as complex systems that can be decomposed into a 
hierarchy of loosely coupled subsystems with standardized interfaces to assist with the 
design of those systems (Baldwin and Clark, 2000). While simple systems can be 
relatively easy to design and acquire, more complex systems can have multiple levels of 
granularity (i.e., the size, details, and interconnectivity of the elements).  Many examples 
of successful modular approaches can be taken from the consumer goods, commercial 
sectors, automotive industries, and academia. There has been a rise in the use of 
modularity in sectors such as aeronautical and defense industries as well (Baldwin & 
Clark, 2014). Modularity has an encouraging affect with respect to new product 
development time and product execution, which ultimately leads to faster time to market 
(Danese & Filippini, 2013).  
Modularity has proven to help reduce costs, accelerate innovation, and improve time 
to market. From computers to automobiles, there has been tremendous success with the 
use of a modular strategy. Using modular components, for example, Dell was able to 
achieve lower costs, higher sales volumes, faster delivery, and greater variety (Ro, Liker, 
& Fixson, 2007). Modularity has proven to increase product variation and also shorten 
delivery times (Ulrich, 1995). Modularity in defense military systems design has shown 
advantages in terms of adaptability, resilience, and cost savings (Dasch & Gorsich, 2016). 
12 
 
Modularity has been perceived as a strategy to better manage lifecycle costs and as well 
as project complexity while increasing the ability and adaptability of systems. (Heydari & 
Dalili, 2015).  
“Common architecture, standard product portfolio elements, and a systematic 
approach can help companies cut costs and boost top-line growth” (Bannasch, Grüntges, 
Rossi, & Weig, 2018). An additional benefit is that an organization could increase 
competition within its suppliers, and it benefits the manufacturer to not be limited to only 
one supplier. Modularity has helped improve supplier integration by creating a common 
language to simplify communication (Lorenzi and Lello, 2001). “For the Defence sector, 
adopting open standards is no longer just a preference; it has become a central driver in 
system design.” (Clark, 2019). The ability to communicate clearly, frequently, and with 
lesser effort (Galvin and Morkel, 2001) has resulted in a higher level of information 
being exchanged and thus helping towards building a stronger relationship between the 
customer and supplier. In another study, Jacobs further found that modularity has led to 
an increase in quality with the increase in standardization of modules and led to 
additional benefits such as increased cost savings due to less testing and ease of 
technology insertion. (Jacobs, Vickery, & Droge, 2007).

2.4 Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA) 
Almost all current and future defense programs within the US Department of Defense 
are currently being acquired using systems engineering processes. An important change 
to this acquisition process is the introduction and use of an open and modular open 
system method known as Modular Open System Approach.  The MOSA focuses on the 
ability to integrate modern technology and products while also ensuring the affordable 
and constant future upgrading of systems and sub-systems to stay ahead in the game. 
“MOSA can be defined as a method to design systems with highly cohesive, loosely 
coupled, and severable modules that DoD can compete separately and acquire from 
independent vendors” (Zimmerman, Ofori, Barrett, Soler, & Harriman, 2018). In fact, the 
DoD has taken an active interest in pursuing the MOSA methodology for its major 
defense acquisition programs looking through its entire life cycle activities and areas of 
opportunity. Analyzing this will hope to support “rapid evolution, faster cycle time, and 
accelerate overall delivery of defense systems” (ODASD, 2017). 
MOSA is considered to be a revolutionary approach that focuses on both the business 
aspect but also the technical aspect of acquisition by focusing on reduction of cost when 
it comes to defense system growth and sustainment. DoD’s 2015 Better Buying Power 
3.0 initiative focuses on the philosophy that adopting and mandating MOSA will help 
bring an increase in innovation and competition, in cost, and in efficiency. The Better

2.7 Adoption of ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 by the DoD to increase inoperability  
The move towards better acquisition reforms by the DoD has led to more non-
governmental and widely acceptable open standards. The DoD acquisition process 
requires programs to use MOSA and react more quickly to technology, threats, and 
mission changes. This has further pushed to provide more interoperability between 
standards. The adoption of the ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 standard, Systems and Software 
Engineering – System Life Cycle Processes was implemented by the DoD. This step has 
allowed the acquisition process to be more streamlined and catered to a more systems 
engineering related standard thus terminating numerous existing military specifications 
and standards. The ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 allows for a universal framework for system 
lifecycles and focuses on a concentrated set of systems engineering processes that are 
used in the concept, expansion, manufacturing, application, support, and retirement 
lifecycle stages (Daly 2017). “Pat Shanahan, the then Deputy Secretary of Defense 
30 
 
overseeing the reorganization of the former chief weapon buyer into two new positions, 
said speed and rapid enhancement of existing systems with emerging technologies is a 
personal goal.” (Parsons 2017). In fact, two new DoD System Engineering Non-
Government Standards were established by the DoD complementing ISO/IEC/IEEE 
15288: 
1. IEEE 15288.1 - IEEE Standard for Application of Systems Engineering on 
Defense Programs  
2. IEEE 15288.2 - IEEE Standard for Technical Reviews and Audits on Defense 
Programs  
Both these standards were published on May 15, 2015 and first used within the DoD 
the following month on June 5, 2015. Both these standards were developed to specifically 
provide guidance on definition, control, and improvement for DoD acquisition projects 
with an SE focus. “The 15288 Standards provide one method to define the acquirer’s 
expectations and requirements for the supplier’s performance of SE processes and 
technical reviews and audits. Thoughtful and proper use of these standards can enhance 
communication and understanding between the acquirer and supplier throughout the 
solicitation process and contract execution.” (DoD Best Practices, 2017). This ties 
directly to one of the five benefits of MOSA set by the DoD which is increased 
interoperability for mission integration. Furthermore, INCOSE in its 2015 Systems

3.3 Hypothesis Overview – H1  - Cost and Schedule Effects  
In order to evaluate possible effects of MOSA on DoD programs, this praxis looked

The GAO’s annual assessment report includes performance, cost, and schedule

MOSA had been introduced by the DoD as the Department was under great pressure

MOSA was mandated under the Better Buying Power 3.0 Guidance that focused on 
“Achieving Dominant Capabilities through Technical Excellence and Innovation,” while

the DoD to address faster innovation and in assisting it to meet cost and schedule targets. 
The DoD acquires its weapon systems through a process called the Defense

5000.01). The three major phases are: 
1. Technology Maturation (Technology Development)  
2. Engineering & Manufacturing Development (Systems Development) 
3. Production & Deployment (Production)

Programs that demonstrate a unit cost increase of fifteen percent or more and programs

Modular open systems could be of great value to the acquisition of defense systems. 
As systems grow more complex, traditional approaches to promote innovation will no

technological products and systems that we design and use today are characterized by 
more and more components and increasingly intricate interdependences, implying

the key focus areas of introducing MOSA is to help work towards solving growing 
complexity and introducing innovative practices. Because the amount of investment in

4.2 Hypothesis Overview – H1 – Cost and Schedule Effects  
For this hypothesis, a data set was reviewed from the 2017 GAO report published by 
the DoD. In this report, there were 86 MDAP programs that were carefully reviewed and 
classified if they had modular aspect to their acquisition process or not. These programs 
were then compared to better understand a relationship to see if programs that used the 
MOSA strategy had a greater project success as compared to programs that did not 
implement the MOSA strategy. A Two Sample T Test was used to determine if both 
populations would lead to some convincing results. There were two data sets that were 
reviewed for this analysis. 
Data Set I – This data set included a collection of all the 86 major defense acquisition 
programs in terms of the total cost spent on the program to date and an overview of how 
the programs performed with respect to the initial planned cost versus actual cost. More 
specifically, the research compared the cost of the first full approximations of the overall 
acquisition costs (in financial year dollars) from the DoD’s 2017 MDAP list. Table 1 in 
44 
 
the Appendix lists the cost comparison along with a percentage change from initial cost 
to current cost. From the 86 MDAP programs, there were 66 programs that used or are 
currently implementing modular or open architecture methods during the life cycle 
process of the program. The remaining 20 programs showed no use of modular 
techniques. This lack of MOSA adherence could be because of factors such as the 
programs were upgrading or increased quantity re-orders and there was no requirement or 
justification to apply modularity practices to such programs. Resulting from this 
investigation, the research observed that of the 66 MDAP modular programs, 46 
programs were over budget with respect to cost from their initial estimate, and 20 
programs were within or on their initial budget. Similarly, of the 20 MDAP Non-modular 
programs, the research found nine programs that were over budget and 11 that were 
within their initial estimate of their program budget. 
Data Set II – In this dataset, the research looked at 57 programs within the 2017 
MDAP list which specifically had detailed one-page summaries in the GAO 2017 Annual 
Weapon System report. The reason these fifty-seven programs were chosen is because at 
the time of the report, 45 of the 57 MDAP’s were between the beginning of the 
developmental cycle and between the preliminary stages of production. The remaining 12 
were future MDAP programs which may later enhance our understanding of acquisition 
reforms. All these 57 programs align with the Better Buying Power 3.0 Interim Release 
which focuses on addressing sound management practices, the use of prototypes, more 
realistic cost estimate measures, and the use of sound systems engineering principles. Of 
the 57 present and future programs that were evaluated, 55 programs stated that they 
intended to encourage competition between vendors throughout the lifecycle of the 
45 
 
system acquisition. The remaining two future programs emphasized that their current 
project plans did not involve or prioritize competition either before or after development 
started. Program offices report they use the following measures to promote competition:  
• Use of modular open architectures, that promotes the use of several modules 
which work together as a cohesive system that can be accomplished as a separate 
sub-system and also be acquired independently from the same or different 
vendors, thus promoting competition and parallel development.   
• Along with the acquisition of the specific system, to also acquire the related 
system specifications and drawings as part of the technical package from the 
vendor. (GAO, 2017). 
• “The two programs that have no current plans to use modularity are the Air 
Force’s B-2 Extremely High Frequency Satellite Communications system and the 
UH-1N Utility Helicopter Replacement program which may or may not change its 
priorities during the lifecycle process as they evolve” (GAO, 2017). 
From the 57 programs with respect to schedule, 23 programs have not yet started and

schedule, out of the 34 programs, 27 programs were found to be over schedule.  This

cost, out of 34 programs, 19 programs are over budget which equates to about 56% of the 
programs.  
The findings of this phase of the research, while very interesting, were ultimately 
insufficient to statistically support the hypotheses and fully isolate the MOSA effect from 
its confounding variables. Strategies such as the application of lean, agile, six sigma,

commonality, specific systems engineering methods, model-based systems engineering 
initiatives, or even a change of leadership over the term of the project could be 
contributing confounders to the success or failure of a programs.

Overall, the results show that currently it is impossible with the published MDAP

interoperability, similar to the lines of MOSA.

initiative with targeting cost, focusing on affordability, and promoting a repository of

These include, in the Navy, Littoral Combat Ships, Common Control Station, 
Multi-Operator Control Unit, Fire Scout Tactical Control Station, and the Unmanned 
Surface Vehicle programs of record. In the Army, they include that Joint Multi Role 
Rotorcraft, Universal Ground Control Station, and the Bi-directional Remote Video 
Terminal, and in the Air Force, the ADM Global Hawk GCS Upgrade, and the Block 50 
Ground Control Station projects (Ernst, 2016). 
The conclusion that can be drawn from the UCS initiative is that there is an uphill 
task to develop specific guidelines and standards that will support interoperability of 
systems which will give the ability for military equipment or groups to operate in 
concurrence with each other. These findings clearly show that some of MOSA’s ideas are 
taking hold in the aviation realm and are here to stay. The development of these standards 
has not yet however allowed for increased interchangeability by different military groups 
or for them to work together and that is where there is a need for further development.  
 
2. Open Mission Systems (OMS) Initiative 
The goal for the Open Mission Systems initiative is to create a non-proprietary 
60 
 
open architecture to enable affordability and to drive a defense acquisition model that fits 
the new world of acquiring defense systems. The OMS is one of the initiatives that goes 
hand in hand with the MOSA principles. The benefits OMS is trying to achieve are very 
similar to what MOSA intends to accomplish.  “Adopting open architectures in systems 
that the Defense Department buys from industry can reduce costs and facilitate 
competition.” The integration of the MOSA strategy is to ensure that there is a common 
starting point for various departments to leverage open and modular systems effectively 
as well as share common principles and successes (Bratton, 2019).  
With the directive of the Better Buying Power 3.0 and other governmental 
guidelines, OMS will help reduce development and life cycle cost while providing a 
parallel benefit of easy upgrading and expanding current defense capabilities. As per 
(Tokar 2017), the OMS architecture will deliver benefits such as increased 
interoperability, affordable technical refresh, streamlined integration and an opportunity 
for service reuse. “Almost 75 percent of Defense Department acquisition strategies 
implement open systems architecture across all services and agencies.” (Pellerin, 2014). 
The Open Mission Systems standard (OMS 2015) was developed by the US Air 
Force, with the intention of focusing on service architecture concepts. The main focus of 
their work was to provide an ability for systems to interface with subsystems through the 
use of an Avionics Service Bus. The OMS specification helps with the better 
understanding of specific requirements and developing robust compliance strategies. 
“The Air Force is expanding its Open Mission Systems standard because it is working so 
well, Lt. Gen. Robert Otto, deputy chief of staff for intelligence, surveillance and 
reconnaissance” (Whittle, 2015). The Air Force’s goal is integrating the competencies of 
61 
 
the OMS standard to speed up the advancement in aviation systems.

The outdated strategy of relying on single source suppliers to manufacture huge

OMS is not a strategy to replace systems engineering efforts but instead meant to

Common Architecture etc. The OMS tactic helps by providing a starting place to address

government wants to achieve – an open architectural standard promoting streamlined 
mission integration, empowering affordability, and enabling service reuse and

3. Future Airborne Capability Environment (FACE) Approach 
The FACE approach was developed in 2010 to focus on developing an open 
aviation strategy for military avionics platform types. A decade later, FACE is a real time 
software group that includes defense personnel, industry suppliers, and academia all 
working together to build an affordable software system that drives the rapid integration 
of COTS and promotes innovation. The goal of FACE is to standardize open system 
approaches in avionic systems and being in common solutions. The FACE approach 
allows an opportunity for the US government and its vendors to work together and 
develop open system solutions and best practices specific for aviation systems. 
“The US DoD continues to leverage open architecture solutions that help improve 
speed, flexibility, and costs. The FACE approach is one of those methods through which 
open system standards are leveraged and avionics hardware is integrated to ensure 
operations are more secure, portable, and strong” (Seawright, 2020). The FACE technical 
standard was built on the motivation of the advances in mobile devices using common 
environments. “With the creation of the FACE Technical Standard, the DoD and our 
industry partners in the FACE Consortium are setting the direction for software 
portability and interoperability. This standard will enable reduced costs and speed 
delivery of technical innovation to the Warfighting community.” (Tescher 2012).

4.4.4 Global Overview  
This praxis’ focus on other country’s adaptation to modularity is essential to its

of commercial or defense interest. “In general, MOSA initiatives are adopted for three 
primary reasons: (1) to avoid being left behind as others find new opportunities, (2) to 
take advantage of new methods to improve internal corporate efficiency, as well as to (3) 
increase market share and increase profits” (Guertin, 2018). 
The United States being the forefront in innovation and the application of the 
MOSA strategy has inspired other governments and defense organizations to adopt 
modular and open strategies for their defense systems as they also face similar issues.

Some of the early adopters of the MOSA strategy have been the UK MOD, Australian

1. MOSA & the Canadian Procurement Strategy 
The strides and developments made by the US DoD have led to studies and 
similar strategies applied globally (Christianson, 2018). The CAF and government have 
had similar problems with their defense systems procurement programs failing to meet 
schedule and cost targets (Stone, 2015). The significance of getting defense procurement 
strategies right cannot be understated and the lack of ability to deliver and maintain 
defense systems possess a great threat and risk not only to the government and defense 
organizations but also compromises the defense capabilities of the country. While smaller 
Canadian programs have been acquired in more timely fashion, a lot of the larger 
acquisitions have faced major delays and cost overruns, similar to the US DoD. These 
challenges have led to political, media, governmental, and public concerns with respect to 
the departments being able to appropriately manage Canadian taxpayers funds (Auger, 
2016). While it is acknowledged that flaws within Canada’s defense procurement process 
leading to project delays and cost overruns are a “perennial burden on both government 
and industry,” (Stone 2015), the Praxis will highlight the effects and evolving challenges 
associated with global defense procurement strategies.  
As costs associated with defense systems keep rising, the MOSA philosophy 
focuses on leveraging IT trends and open system architectures to improve innovation and 
incorporate new technologies all the while reducing costs. The Canadian government 
made some aggressive moves to improve their defense acquisition strategies through 
launching a centralized shipbuilding strategy in 2010 and a defense procurement strategy 
69 
 
to increase efficiency and accountability (Auger, 2016). 
The CAF has acknowledged that modular and open strategies show promise of 
reducing life cycle costs and incorporate reduced risk while enhancing flexibility and 
adaptability while procuring defense systems (Christianson, 2018). Many Canadian 
defense system platforms such as the Sea King, Aurora, the CF-18 Hornet, and Halifax 
class frigates are all in service after three decades and well beyond their intended 
lifespans. Such a trend is further exacerbated by the fact that the procurement process 
itself can add an additional 10 to 15 years between identification of requirements and 
delivery; meaning that a defined requirement from identification could be expected to 
remain relevant for as much as half a century if no planned upgrade is incorporated 
(Williams 2006). Since defense systems come with a significant investment, it is 
understandable that many of these systems are active and over their intended lifespan. 
However, with the pace at which technology is evolving, it is essential that defense 
systems of today are more agile and flexible and that they support the diverse missions, 
threats, operating environments, and changing governmental policies. Recent history has 
witnessed a near exponential increase in computing and networking capabilities, a trend 
which is fully anticipated to continue as suggested by concepts such as Moore’s Law and 
Butter’s Law which argue computing and communications capacity double every 24 and 
9 months respectively (Simonite, 2020). Moore’s Law anticipated half a decade ago that 
technology in transistors per chip would double every two years and served as a rule of 
innovation as products and services advanced. Fifty years later, the law still holds good. 
Butter’s Law predicted the amount of data in a fiber cable would double every nine 
months and this led to work in optical fiber cables. Analysis across multiple weapon 
70 
 
classes has demonstrated that per unit costs have increased significantly faster than the 
rate of general inflation or contemporary Gross Domestic Products, in many cases at rates 
exceeding 5-10 percent per year or higher for technologically intensive capabilities 
(Kirkpatrick 2004). This has led to a decrease in purchasing power for defense programs 
around the world and has also led to a focus on platforms being more adaptable rather 
than frequently replacing defense systems platforms as per traditional approaches. 
 
2. U.K. Ministry of Defense’s Approach to Modularity 
The United States and Canada are not the only nations to pursue modular and 
open architecture initiatives. Observing the early signs of progress of the US DoD such as 
Victory (US Army), the Future Airborne Capability Environment (US Navy), and the 
Open System Approach, the UK Ministry of Defence has been prioritizing similar 
approaches with the Generic Vehicle Architecture (GVA) program.  
The United States has always been the vanguard when it comes to defense and 
defense spending. Understanding the success and struggles of other countries who have 
also adopted open systems and modularity is essential as it gives a further understanding 
if defense organizations are able to better measure and justify a modular strategy. With 
heightened implementation of MOSA there will be better data to quantify how 
organizations are able to better qualify defense systems and whether success lies in 
systems and platforms, sub systems, or is just constrained to off the shelf products. There 
lies an opportunity to measure defense programs and compare their project performance 
success to defense systems across the globe. This comparison will further help reiterate if 
modularity and open systems are actually delivering the benefits that they had intended 
71 
 
to.  The benefits of MOSA are clear. It helps with easier integration, enhanced flexibility, 
faster upgrades, and great long-term value (Clark, 2019).  
The UK MOD has made great strides in multiple projects since April 2018 in 
including modular and system approaches within their acquisition projects. The 
introduction of the mandatory Open Architecture Key User Requirement, developing of 
the new open architecture vehicles, and the Land Open System Architecture initiative 
have all been positive steps towards a more agile defense strategy (Clark, 2019).  UK has 
further articulated such objectives within its System of Systems Open System Strategy 
and has begun implementation of MOSA as part of the both the New Generation 
Submarine Command System and the General Vehicle Architecture for Land Vehicles 
(Secretary of State for Defence, 2012). The latest document, “National Security Through 
Technology: Technology, Equipment, and Support for UK Defence and Security” has re-
accentuated that the UK's defense and security requirements must be acquired through 
open competition from the domestic and global marketplace, and through buying 
commercial products such as COTS which use open standards (Ministry of Defence, 
2012). The MOD has also published similar standards to MOSA called Systems of 
Systems Approach (SOSA), that largely focuses on acquiring defense capabilities 
through the use of modular and open systems. “SOSA offers nine design principles for 
decision makers to guide behaviors throughout the acquisition lifecycle and all nine have 
the potential to be met through the adoption of an open approach” (Henshaw 2019). The 
GVA was built with the Royal Army’s concern that the nature of the threat is constantly 
evolving and by the time the army has adapted highly technological capabilities into their 
vehicles, the enemy has already countered and advanced their technology. Thus, the 
72 
 
MOD focused on a more open and modular response to better address the system 
upgrades and integration challenges.  
Using an open system approach and common interface, the MOD was able to 
successfully integrate a common functionality to increase land vehicle effectiveness thus 
enhancing adaptability and introducing plug and play into their land defense systems. 
“The MoD has set a world-leading example of how to manage defense procurements in 
the face of new threats as the enemy becomes increasingly shrewd in leveraging their 
asymmetric capabilities. With GVA and Def Stan 23-09, the MoD has a procurement 
strategy for delivering military vehicles that are coherently designed, while maximizing 
utility and enabling field command to get the best logistically out of its assets. The 
architecture empowers field command to plug-and-play so as to adjust to rapidly 
changing threat profiles.” (Hunt 2011).  
From the above discussion, it is evident that modularity and open systems are 
playing a big part in shaping the procurement strategies for organizations and countries 
outside the U.S. and that like the US, other governments have mandated modular 
approaches.  The research did not reveal, however, how these other governments were 
measuring the program performance effects of modularity only that the effects were 
positively felt. 
 
4.5 Hypothesis Overview – H5 & H6 – MOSA’s impact on the commercial sector  
The reason the praxis is including MOSA’s effects on commercial vendors adopting 
MOSA standards is because during the literature review, it was evident that the open 
systems mandate by the US Government will require a new way of working with its 
73 
 
suppliers. The US DoD has been too reliant on single source vendors. “A recent GAO 
report revealed that the average acquisition for a major Defense weapons system 
experienced a 38% cost growth from original estimates and a 27-month schedule 
overrun” (GAO, 2012). Vendor lock often leads to proprietary data and intellectual 
property rights controlled by the supplier and any changes or upgrades came at a higher 
cost due to the ownership. With the execution of MOSA, the government has made it a 
fair playing field, controls the rights, and prevents misuse from a single vendor. One of 
the ways MOSA is trying to better adapt to this is by creating increased competition 
within suppliers and vendors of the defense systems. The DoD is demanding that its 
suppliers adopt to a more flexible and open platform so that its defense systems can be 
used for multiple missions needs instead of highly concentrated needs. The MOSA 
strategy has thus led to the US DoD ending reliance on single source vendors and 
working towards increasing competition within its ecosystem. “A recent study by the 
Center for Strategic and International Studies estimates that from 2001 to 2015, 17,000 
companies ceased to be prime vendors for DoD.” (McCormick, 2018). This is a positive 
effect that is allowing the DoD to increase competition amongst its vendors and thus 
driving better affordability within its projects. 
The MOSA methodology warrants a better understanding of how the DoD is focusing 
on transforming its acquisition process to be more adaptable. With the increased spending 
over the years on defense systems and the continuous trend of defense systems being over 
budget or over schedule, there was a strong review of programs by the DoD and the 
Government Accountability Office to ensure that these problems will not persist. 
The HForce by Airbus is one of the latest generation air weapon systems that meets 
74 
 
the requirements of a defense system of the future. The HForce is a modular and flexible 
helicopter that has been developed with off the shelf solution needs to cater to multiple 
mission needs. From intelligence to surveillance to combat recovery, defense agencies 
can gain flexibility in their use of systems like these as well as transition to another 
mission need quickly. “Airbus Helicopters recently changed the situation of single 
purpose helicopters to releasing helicopters with multi-purpose mission computer 
platform such as HForce that can be fitted into any military version of Airbus’s 
commercial helicopters, including the H125M, H145M, and H225M helicopters, 
converting any commercial helicopter into an armed, battle-ready unit for military 
operations. Designed as a modular and incremental system, HForce offers a large choice 
of weapons (air-to-air, air-to-ground, ballistic or guided) to support any operational 
need.” (Downing 2018). Similar to the HForce defense system, the trend of suppliers 
delivering complex, modern, and modular systems are here to stay and will support the 
vision of defense organizations.

5.2 MOSA Discussion  
From the overall praxis a better understanding on the reason behind the mandate on 
the MOSA strategy and what it is all about can be gained. Open systems and modularity 
carry a large interest due to the potential of lower program costs, increased integration of 
COTS, and ease of integration. While the government has taken multiple steps to address 
the failure to meet cost and schedule targets, MOSA brings a promising turn on assisting 
with defense acquisition. Since the Better Buying Power 3.0 mandate in 2015, not a 
single study has looked at the overall MOSA performance from a Return of Investment 
perspective. Sooner or later, the DoD will want to understand how the MOSA strategy is 
performing and whether it is achieving its intended goals. From a project level, MOSA 
has successfully increased industry collaboration, developed consortiums that included 
the best minds and skills globally working towards a goal of increased modular and open 
system integration, and increased buy in on all the aspects of the architecture  
(Worthington, 2012). 
Some issues the DoD might run into while trying to evaluate this would be the similar 
problem this praxis intended to achieve initially which was through a quantitative multi-
year analysis and whether programs that use the MOSA strategy are performing better in 
terms of achieving cost and schedule targets versus programs that currently do not 
81 
 
employ the use of MOSA. At what level and detail will the evaluation take place, 
platform, system, sub-system, service, COTS integration? Whether a distinction will 
occur between the Army, Navy, and the Air Force or will all the programs be combined 
under the DoD umbrella? 
With all that said, this praxis contributes a synoptic view of MOSA not only within 
the DoD but also its impact on a global foreign military defense and commercial 
organizations standpoint. Thus, this work is a unique and valuable contribution. This 
praxis compiled a lot of various examples and summarizes that MOSA as a strategy has 
had a lot of great impact to project performance from various branches within the DoD. 
Along with examples, there are a lot of positive case studies with cost and time savings in 
projects, but they are still not clearly available or stored in a separate repository for better 
access. An opportunity exists to share proven practices and lessons learned so different 
teams do not repeat similar mistakes and help the organization succeed as one. 
From an overall praxis view, the research objectives and the problem statements have 
been addressed through the different discussions and analysis. As per the problem 
statement, “An organized search for the representation of evidence of the success of the 
MOSA strategy is necessary to sustain and characterize MOSA and what about it is 
working .  Such an effort is also needed to develop recommendations that will enable its 
success.” The praxis was able to demonstrate multiple views of MOSA. Modularity, open 
systems, and defense acquisition processes are all described through the literature search 
and the body of knowledge. The praxis was able to achieve its objective of introducing 
the concept of MOSA to its readers, analyze the impact of MOSA across the different 
departments in the US DoD as well as other sectors through the use of examples and 
82 
 
literature compilation, and finally demonstrate the emergence of MOSA as a strategy that 
will transform the acquisition of defense systems.  
From the research and literature review, there is clearly a limitation on the 
understanding of modularity and openness in the acquisition process. The DoD scope of 
MOSA methodology needs to include not only program managers but also a larger 
ecosystem to appropriately adopt MOSA strategies. “MOSA must become commonplace, 
with design patterns, instantiations, tool use, measurement, etc. Elements of MOSA 
practice are found but are not habitually documented” (Zimmerman, Ofori, Barrett, Soler, 
& Harriman, 2018). Obviously, there is a need to include long term goals and strategies 
specific to MOSA and its principles. From this, there is a need to specify, educate, and 
understand the implementation of MOSA currently and to integrate future adoption with 
MOSA standards, principles, and a more robust chain of communication. In order to gain 
the maximum advantage from modular systems, organizations will need to focus on how 
modularity can work best for their needs and requirements.  (Pakkanen, Juuti, & 
Lehtonen, 2018). The research also revealed a strong requirement to include a phase to 
evaluate modular strategies in the initial planning of the life cycle process along with 
formalized pass-fail criteria so that vendors and the armed forces are on the same page 
when it comes to defense acquisition. The research suggests that another point is to 
implement MOSA specific training. While it is good to have documentation as a 
reference point, it is essential that all the stakeholders get additional training specific to 
MOSA to ensure compliance. Incentives, both positive and negative, should be 
considered to help overcome resistance to change and the perceived risks of 
implementing a new approach. What to measure, how to measure, and with what 
83 
 
measures still remain key challenges. Lastly, the research exposed the need for a formal 
repository showcasing effective knowledge management, case studies, and lessons 
learned from all legacy and current programs that use modularity. One of the best ways to 
describe concepts such as modularity and flexibility is similar to how quality was 
described a few decades ago, “vague and difficult to improve yet critical to 
competitiveness” (Upton, 1995). 
5.3 Other Observations 
Open systems and modularity bring a large appeal due to the potential of lower 
program costs, increased integration of COTS, and ease of integration. However, with 
greater connectivity there will be a higher risk of vulnerability due to the openness of a 
system. None of the literature surveyed mentioned this key detail.  Clearly, there is a need 
for more research into how open systems and modularity can not only facilitate more 
secure defense systems but also help defense programs facilitate technology refresh, 
increase innovation, and enable cost savings. 
To ensure that US DoD keeps its competitive advantage, researchers and strategists 
have followed leading trends in technology such as lean and agile methods, smart 
manufacturing, concurrent engineering, and autonomy. These approaches have failed to 
address the root cause of why the US has fallen behind in fielding new technology 
efficiently and affordably as compared to its competitors. The MOSA initiative helps 
solve this problem by requiring a fresh acquisition model focusing on flexible system 
architectures that allow quick integration of new technologies and to continually 
upgrade systems allowing the US to adapt to new threats under the ever-increasing 
economic pressure. The main goal of MOSA is to increase affordability and improve the 
84 
 
timelines of defense acquisition programs so that they can be mission ready.   
While it is premature to decisively say that MOSA as a strategy is successful in 
achieving all its intended benefits such as enhanced competition with vendors, reduction 
in cycle time and costs, ease of integration, and other similar benefits, early indications 
suggest that the move to modular and open systems through MOSA is on the right path. 
That being said, there is a strong need to expand the use of MOSA and modularity 
across systems, sub-systems, interfaces, and platform architectures. It is critical from the 
above work that there is a need for more development of modular standards and 
assessments. There is substantial prospect for further assistance on modular integrations 
and analysis that will help transition the use of modularity into other branches of the 
government and other industries.   
 
5.4 Limitations  
Modularity, open architecture, and MOSA are all very broad terms and can lead to 
various interpretations. Further, there may be programs within the DoD that have already 
applied some level of modularity or are currently applying modular approaches using 
different terminology. The US DoD is the largest defense engineering organization in the 
world. Due to this, there are a lot of great processes, expertise, and high-level system 
engineers that practice to create large scale, interdependent, and highly complex defense 
systems. MOSA is one behavior that exists to assist with building successful highly 
technological defense systems while trying to balance schedule and cost goals. Due to the 
limitations placed on US DoD programmatic information available to private citizens, not 
all the information needed to study MOSA’s impact on programs is readily available. 
85 
 
This research also does not indicate that MOSA is the only factor that could affect 
program acquisition. While there may be multiple confounding variables impacting a 
defense system’s acquisition and its acquisition cycle can be multiyear (and usually 
consist of multiple sub-systems), the goal of this research was to provide insight on 
MOSA, the lack of literature and scholarly articles on this topic, and to begin to close that 
gap.  Wirth respect to quantifying if open systems can prove cost savings quantitatively, 
this remains a challenge since there many factors in acquisition programs in parallel, data 
for potential cost saving for alternative procurement approaches does not exist and cost 
savings are primarily just estimated for a business justification (Worthington, 2012).  The 
goal was also to shed light on how the measurement of MOSA’s effects can be done 
better so that the positive influence of MOSA can be effectively assessed and promoted. 
5.5 Contributions to the Body of Knowledge  
Currently there is very limited research pertaining to how effective the MOSA 
strategy has been in the DoD acquisition process. Until now there has not been any 
significant work summarizing the efforts of MOSA from a holistic view. From the 
research and literature review, there is a limitation on the understanding of modularity 
and openness in the acquisition process. This work adds to the body of knowledge of 
MOSA, modularity, and open systems. As the DoD continues to adopt MOSA, one of the 
biggest challenges this praxis has led to identify is that while there is plenty of empirical 
evidence to suggest MOSA’s success, it is currently highly difficult to actually measure 
MOSA and its effects. What to measure, how to measure, and with what metrics to 
measure remain the key challenge. Overall, there is a strong need for an assessment 
methodology and tools to better gauge the understanding and impacts modularity has on 
86 
 
its programs.  
Some additional contributions this praxis is with respect to the bodies of knowledge 
in the fields of Engineering Management, Project Management and Systems Engineering 
disciplines. Since the DoD heavily relies on the use of robust Systems Engineering  and 
Engineering methods, these practices are considered key contributors for successful 
system acquisition. Furthermore, the acquisition process is generally managed by project 
managers and the praxis will be of high interest to program managers managing open and 
modular system integration into their programs.  
Hopefully, the data this praxis analyzed and collected will serve as a background to 
construct a more robust framework involving defense acquisition processes which will 
help reduce the cost and schedule overrun of all large human-engineered systems.  
5.6 Recommendations for Future Research  
Future research may include limiting the sample of studied programs to only certain 
stages and size of acquisition programs. Furthermore, as new acquisition programs use 
MOSA with a goal of increasing innovation and implementing COTS, there will be more 
data pertaining to the performance of MOSA and that will help in furthering the study of 
MOSA and its impact on major system lifecycles. Better measures and stronger modular 
assessment tools are needed to empower acquisition teams and leaders to better design 
and make decisions on programs ensuring that programs better meet cost and schedule 
targets. 
Subsequent work could also focus on a comparison between whether the technical 
aspect or the business aspect of the MOSA strategy is a more effective approach. It could 
also be a combination of both the approaches that make it an attractive acquisition 
87 
 
strategy. Similarly, an important system engineering question is in which part of the 
lifecycle process should MOSA be introduced? Another interesting aspect is studying the 
Defense Acquisition Life Cycle Compliance Chart - DoDI 5000.02 which lays out all the 
decision points and milestones and understand where MOSA will have the greatest 
impact and understand if the chart should be modified to ensure programs are being 
measured to modular and open system capabilities. Research is also needed to determine 
how modular designs affect the verification and validation steps.  
Future work could include detailed MOSA, cost, and schedule metrics on legacy 
projects to compare and analyze benefits of modularity and openness. “There is a clear 
need for support regarding the governance, culture, and strategic issues relating to the 
engineering design of modular systems as an operational mode” (Pakkanen, Juuti & 
Lehtonen 2018). One vital area of research is security and safety with respect to open and 
modular designs in defense systems. The shift from controlled platforms to open systems 
while providing greater performance and cost improvements, also has posed a significant 
data and technology challenge. With the advancement of remote and global teams and the 
boost to open system architectures, managing the balance between security, complexity, 
ethical, and legal challenges in defense systems is critical to understand and set 
guidelines to ensure there is no misuse. Modularity is not a one-time approach but a 
constant updating of standards and interfaces to ensure that the DoD’s acquisition is 
efficient and ahead of the technology curve on a global scale. Lastly, there is a need to 
quantify modularity in terms of hardware, software, and platforms and associate all this 
information to real value so as to understand how much modularity is helping and saving 
in the long run. With better metrics and stronger modular assessment tools, acquisition 
88 
 
teams and leaders will be able to better design and make decisions on programs ensuring 
that programs perform better using what is likely a tailored MOSA strategy.  
5.7 Conclusion  
The development and acquisition of defense systems is a complex process. As 
discussed in the praxis, there are many examples from commercial, academic, and 
governmental organizations that demonstrate modularity and open systems have proven 
to be successful. MOSA was introduced with the strategy to help combat ineffective 
acquisition processes and support those shortcomings by working towards a strategy that 
encourages systems to be able to evolve with mission needs and that can be more 
affordable than replacing older systems that are running well over their intended life 
cycles. Enhancing competition, expediting technology refresh, enabling cost savings from 
a design and development aspect, and improving standardization by facilitating data 
sharing, are all the benefits MOSA was intended to target. Through the efficient 
incorporation of modular and open standards, open systems will accelerate the acquisition 
life cycle while accommodating for rapidly changing demands and leveraging concurrent 
innovation to address cost and schedule factors. From the GAO reports, it is suspected 
that the main leading factor for programs that are not meeting cost and schedule targets is 
due to underestimation of the initial costs. The dearth of MOSA data at this time 
prohibited statistical testing, however qualitative analysis and early indications indicate 
that project performance is improving with the use of the MOSA strategy over the 
historical acquisition approach. DoD acquisitions are prone to risk and uncertainty. This 
uncertainty leads to an escalation on cost and time of the acquisition process. It is vital to 
highlight that MOSA is not the end result but rather an approach that can enable 
89 
 
innovation and is one of the many procurement strategies that can help with the DoD 
acquisition problem.
