# Burden Sharing via Modular Open Systems Approaches: A Collaborative Path to Affordable Mass

**Source:** CSIS Analysis, Published December 10, 2024
**Authors:** Gregory Sanders and Audrey Aldisert

## Core Problem & Context
The war in Ukraine exposed critical vulnerabilities in the U.S. defense industrial base (DIB), including **supply chain fragility and a lack of surge capacity**. In an era of great power competition, particularly with China's manufacturing might, the U.S. cannot address these challenges alone. Ramping up production requires **allied burden-sharing**, a bipartisan priority. However, international defense cooperation is inherently difficult.

## The Solution: Modular Open Systems Approaches (MOSA)
MOSA is a design strategy for creating adaptable systems with standardized interfaces that any authorized vendor can use. It is analogous to **Android's open ecosystem versus Apple's closed system**.

### Key Benefits of MOSA:
*   **Enhanced Interoperability:** Allows "plug-and-play" integration of subsystems from different vendors, including foreign ones, into a standardized system. This is critical for coalition operations.
*   **Simplified Exportability:** Makes it easier to design systems for international partners by allowing the removal or swapping of modules containing sensitive technology, reducing proliferation risks.
*   **Increased Acquisition Agility:** Enables faster incorporation of updates and new technology across systems, reducing development times and costs.
*   **Leverages Allied Strengths:** Allows nations to specialize in niche areas of the DIB, creating a **"production web"** that avoids duplication and leverages comparative advantages.

## MOSA and the National Defense Industrial Strategy (NDIS)
The NDIS identifies key U.S. DIB constraints that MOSA directly addresses:
1.  **Suboptimal Interoperability:** MOSA helps standardize systems across diverse vendors and regulations.
2.  **Inflexible Acquisition:** MOSA is explicitly called for in the NDIS to improve efficiency and scalability.
3.  **Failure to Design for Exportability:** MOSA simplifies the process of sharing technology with allies early in development.

## Practical Application: The Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) Program
The U.S. Air Force's CCA program aims to field **at least 1,000 affordable, uncrewed aircraft** to act as force multipliers. MOSA is the cornerstone for international partnership in this program.

*   **Pathway for Cooperation:** Increment two of the CCA program is designed for direct ally and partner engagement, with MOSA serving as the chief entry point.
*   **Open Architectures:** Government Reference Architectures like the **Agile Mission Suite (AMS) GRA** and **Autonomy Government Reference Architecture (A-GRA)** provide the standardized frameworks for international contributions.
*   **Potential Areas for Cooperation:** Includes software development, sensor integration, mission systems, and potentially sustaining competition through cost-sharing.
*   **Operational Goal:** Achieve **technological interoperability**, enabling the handoff of control of autonomous systems between coalition partners to enhance mission readiness.

## Challenges & Recommendations for Implementation
*   **Overclassification:** A major complaint from allies (e.g., Japan) is U.S. overclassification of information. The USAF is working to declassify aspects of MOSA standards.
*   **Lack of Visibility & Incentives:** Allies often lack awareness of how MOSA applies to cooperation. Early, deliberate engagement is critical.
*   **Collective Action Problem:** Requires a new business model from the DOD and industry to overcome.
*   **Recommendation for Allies:** Familiarize themselves with key standards (AMS GRA, A-GRA) **now** in preparation for CCA increment two. Early adoption of MOSA in domestic systems lays the groundwork for future interoperability and cooperation.

## Key Quote
> "Deliberate consideration of partner needs and their threat environments will significantly increase incentives for the adoption of common open architectures and for creating a production web capable of building affordable mass."

## Conclusion
MOSA provides a tangible framework for allied burden-sharing, transforming cooperation from a challenge into a strategic advantage. By enabling a **production web** of specialized, interoperable systems, it offers a path to achieving **affordable mass** and strengthening collective defense against peer competitors. The CCA program represents a critical near-term opportunity to put this approach into practice.
