# Open Development Standards Are Key to Keeping the US Military on Top

**Source:** Defense News Opinion, December 9, 2024
**Authors:** Nick Guertin (Assistant Secretary of the Navy for Research, Development and Acquisition) and Jacob Glassman (Senior Adviser)

## Core Argument
The U.S. Department of Defense must further embrace Modular Open System Architecture (MOSA) as a critical warfighting capability to maintain technological superiority on the 21st-century battlefield.

## The Problem: A Rigid System vs. a Dynamic Battlefield
- Technologically Dynamic Threat: Conflicts in the Middle East and Eastern Europe show adversaries can rapidly field new technologies (e.g., counter-navigation, electronic warfare) that neutralize existing systems.
- Legacy Disadvantage: The DoD's rigid acquisition system and legacy technical architectures hinder the ability to quickly adapt and field new capabilities.
- Industrial Base Constraints: The shift from a WWII manufacturing powerhouse to a service-based economy limits munitions production capacity.

## The Solution: Modular Open System Architecture (MOSA)
- Definition: A mission system architecture using widely practiced, consensus-based standards (e.g., Future Airborne Capability Environment (FACE)).
- Key Benefits:
  - Rapid Capability Transition: Allows for the swift integration of new functions and technologies into existing platforms.
  - Industrial Base Expansion: Enables distributed production by allowing small businesses and non-traditional contractors to produce system modules, scaling capacity for munitions.
  - Cross-Service Collaboration: Reduces redundant development and allows services to share and transition capabilities across platforms.

## Evidence and Current Implementation
- Historical Precedent: During WWII, capabilities like radar, sonar, and chaff were rapidly adapted and introduced during the conflict.
- Modern Examples:
  - Hypersonic Weapons: The Navy, Army, and Air Force are aligning to the Weapon Open System Architecture (WOSA).
  - Next-Gen Air Dominance: The Navy and Air Force are partnering by aligning joint architectures to share mission system capabilities.
- Goal: Deploy these architectures in R&D and test platforms to enable rapid transition to Programs of Record (PoR) without major redesign.

## Call to Action
The authors urge the DoD to:
1. Further align and standardize architectures across combat systems portfolios.
2. View MOSA as a critical combat capability essential for 21st-century warfare.
3. Learn from current global conflicts to prepare for future high-tech battlefields.

> "MOSA is an answer to these critical elements of warfare, enabling a rapid sharing of capability, reduction of redundant development, and the distribution of our industrial base."
